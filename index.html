What Students Need from a Writing Tool

Student writing has specific, recurring problem patterns. Understanding them helps you fix your essays more efficiently — and understand why certain corrections come up again and again.

The errors that cost students marks

  • Subject-verb agreement: "Me and my friend goes to school" — should be "My friend and I go to school." This single error signals weak grammar control to any academic marker.
  • Apostrophe misuse: "Its a good point" instead of "It's a good point." Apostrophe errors are among the most penalised in academic writing.
  • "Should of" / "Could of": These should always be "should have" and "could have." Common in student essays and immediately noticeable to lecturers.
  • Comma splices: Joining two complete sentences with just a comma. "I finished the report, it was due tomorrow" needs either a conjunction or a full stop.
  • Tense inconsistency: Switching between past and present tense within the same essay — a very common issue when writing long documents under time pressure.

CheckMyGrammar catches all of these. For students who paste their essays before submission, it's the equivalent of having a proofreader on call — at no cost.

Common professional writing mistakes

  • Redundant phrases: "At this point in time" (just say "now"), "in order to" (just say "to"), "end result" (just say "result").
  • Wrong word confusion: "Effect" vs "affect", "complement" vs "compliment", "principal" vs "principle" — errors that spell checkers miss because the words are spelled correctly.
  • Passive voice overuse: "The report was completed by the team" vs "The team completed the report." Active voice is clearer and more direct.
  • Missing punctuation: A missing comma can genuinely change the meaning of a sentence — and in a legal or financial document, that matters.

How to Fix Your Text Effectively — A Step-by-Step Approach

  1. Write your first draft without worrying about errors. Trying to write and proofread simultaneously slows you down and produces worse results on both fronts.
  2. Read it out loud once. Your ears catch awkward phrasing that your eyes skip over. If a sentence is hard to say, it probably needs rewriting.
  3. Paste into CheckMyGrammar. Let the tool identify spelling, grammar and style issues automatically.
  4. Work through the errors one at a time. Click each highlighted word, read the explanation, apply the fix or ignore it if it's intentional.
  5. Re-read the corrected version. Sometimes fixing one sentence creates a new issue. A final read-through catches anything remaining.
